Given your equipment and goal, I’d use a **3-day strength structure**:

- **1 heavy full-body gym session**
- **2 home full-body sessions**
- Rucking/walking and kettlebell swings as conditioning

This is enough to preserve or build muscle while cutting, provided the sets are genuinely challenging.

## Weekly structure

| Day       | Training                                |
| --------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Monday    | Home Strength A                         |
| Tuesday   | Walking or easy rucking                 |

# Home Strength A

Focus: pull-ups, unilateral legs, pressing and hamstrings.

### 1. Pull-ups

**4 sets × 5–10 reps**

Stop with approximately 1–2 good reps remaining.

When you can do 10 clean reps across every set:

- Add weight using a backpack.
- Slow the eccentric to 3–4 seconds.
- Pause at the top.
- Use chest-to-bar pull-ups.

### 2. Bulgarian split squat

**4 sets × 8–15 reps per leg**

Hold the kettlebell goblet-style or suitcase-style.

Because 12 kg may be relatively light for you:

- Lower for 3 seconds.
- Pause at the bottom.
- Elevate your front foot slightly.
- Perform 1.5 reps.
- Increase the repetitions.

Unilateral exercises make a limited weight much more useful because one leg absorbs most of the load.

### 3. Push-up variation

**4 sets × 8–20 reps**

Choose a variation that brings you close to failure within that range:

- Regular push-up
- Feet-elevated push-up
- Suspension push-up
- Weighted backpack push-up
- Ring-style push-up using the suspension straps
- Archer push-up

Once ordinary push-ups exceed 20–25 comfortable repetitions, they become more conditioning than strength work.

### 4. Suspension hamstring curl

**3 sets × 8–15 reps**

Keep your hips elevated throughout the movement.

Progress toward:

- Single-leg suspension curls
- Nordic curl eccentrics
- Full Nordic curls

### 5. Kettlebell overhead press

**3 sets × 6–12 reps per side**

This fills a major gap that push-ups do not cover: **vertical pressing**.

Use a strict press rather than turning every repetition into a push press.

### 6. Suspension body saw or ab rollout

**3 sets × 8–15 reps**

Alternatives:

- Hanging knee raise
- Hanging leg raise
- Hollow-body hold
- Suitcase carry

# Home Strength B

Focus: horizontal pulling, hinge strength, shoulders and single-leg control.

### 1. Suspension row

**4 sets × 8–15 reps**

Make it harder by:

- Moving your feet farther forward.
- Elevating your feet.
- Pausing with your chest near the handles.
- Performing one-arm assisted rows.

Keep your body rigid rather than turning it into a standing hip thrust.

### 2. Single-leg Romanian deadlift

**4 sets × 8–15 reps per leg**

Hold the kettlebell in the opposite hand from the working leg.

At only 12 kg, use:

- A slow eccentric
- A pause near the bottom
- Greater range of motion
- Light fingertip support for balance so balance does not become the limiting factor

### 3. Pike push-up

**4 sets × 6–15 reps**

This gives you another vertical pressing pattern and trains the anterior deltoids more strongly than ordinary push-ups.

Progression:

1. Pike push-up
2. Feet-elevated pike push-up
3. Wall-assisted handstand push-up partial
4. Full wall-assisted handstand push-up

You do not need to rush toward handstand push-ups. They are cool, but shoulders do not award bonus points for circus credentials.

### 4. Reverse lunge or step-up

**3 sets × 10–15 reps per leg**

Hold the kettlebell goblet-style.

Since Home A already has Bulgarian split squats, keep this variation slightly easier and focus on controlled repetitions.

### 5. Kettlebell swing

**5–10 sets × 10–15 reps**

Rest approximately 45–90 seconds between sets.

Keep this explosive. End the set once:

- The snap becomes slower.
- Your arms start lifting the kettlebell.
- Your lower back begins doing the work.
- The bell no longer floats naturally.

Do not take ballistic movements to ugly muscular failure.

### 6. Suspension face pull or reverse fly

**3 sets × 12–20 reps**

This trains rear delts, scapular control and upper-back musculature that pressing-heavy home programs commonly neglect.

# Full-body gym session

Your gym session should cover what your home equipment cannot load sufficiently.

## 1. Heavy squat pattern

Choose one:

- Back squat
- Front squat
- Hack squat
- Leg press

**3–4 sets × 5–8 reps**

Because your previous squat strength was around 120 kg, a 12 kg kettlebell is unlikely to provide enough bilateral leg stimulus. This is one of the highest-value reasons to retain the gym day.

## 2. Heavy horizontal press

Choose one:

- Barbell bench press
- Dumbbell bench press
- Weighted dips
- Machine chest press

**3–4 sets × 5–10 reps**

If dips feel comfortable and the gym has proper bars, you can do them here. But dips are not mandatory.

## 3. Heavy hip hinge

Choose one:

- Romanian deadlift
- Conventional deadlift
- Trap-bar deadlift
- Hip thrust

**3 sets × 5–10 reps**

I would lean toward Romanian deadlifts because your home setup already provides swings, while heavy RDLs give your hamstrings a more direct loaded stretch.

## 4. Weighted pull-up or heavy row

Choose one primary pull:

- Weighted pull-up
- Chest-supported row
- Cable row
- Barbell row

**3–4 sets × 6–10 reps**

Alternate every few training blocks between vertical and horizontal emphasis.

## 5. Lateral raise

**3 sets × 10–20 reps**

Your home compound pressing trains front delts reasonably well, but side delts often need more direct work.

## 6. Optional arms

Choose one superset:

- Cable curl + triceps pushdown
- Dumbbell curl + overhead triceps extension

**2–3 sets × 8–15 reps**

Optional means optional. Your arms will not evaporate because you skipped cable curls once.

# Are you missing anything without dips?

Not really.

Dips primarily give you:

- Horizontal-to-diagonal pressing
- Chest
- Triceps
- Anterior deltoids
- A deep loaded stretch

You can cover those muscles with:

- Weighted or suspension push-ups
- Gym bench press
- Close-grip push-ups
- Overhead presses
- Pike push-ups

The bigger potential gaps in a home push-up-only program are actually:

1. **Vertical pressing**
2. **Progressive overload**
3. **Side-delt training**
4. **Heavy lower-body loading**

Your kettlebell press and pike push-ups solve the first. Weighted push-ups solve the second. Gym lateral raises solve the third. Your weekly gym squat and RDL solve the fourth.

So no, you do not need to build a sketchy dip setup between two plastic chairs and become a cautionary YouTube Short.

# Conditioning and fat loss

Strength training protects muscle. It does not need to provide all your calorie expenditure.

Use:

### Rucking

**1–3 sessions weekly**

Start around:

- 10–12 kg
- 15–30 minutes
- Comfortable pace
- Flat terrain initially

Increase **duration before load**. Since 12 kg is already 20% of your body weight, you do not need a heavier pack soon.

### Ordinary walking

Aim for roughly **7,000–10,000 total daily steps**, including rucking steps.

You can treat one rucking step as somewhat more costly than one ordinary step, but do not use a precise conversion. Terrain, speed, incline and pack position change the equation substantially.

### Optional swing conditioning

If your Home B swings are not enough, add one separate short session:

```
Every minute for 10 minutes:

10–15 kettlebell swings
Rest for the remaining minute
```

Do not stack hard swing conditioning, hard rucking and heavy leg training every day. Fat loss benefits from expenditure; it does not benefit from inflamed shins and a cooked lower back.

# Progression system

For each exercise, use a repetition range—for example, **8–15 reps**.

When you reach the top of the range for every set with clean technique:

1. Add weight.
2. Choose a harder variation.
3. Increase the range of motion.
4. Slow the eccentric.
5. Add a pause.
6. Add one set only when the other options are unavailable.

Example:

```
Week 1: Bulgarian split squat — 4 × 10
Week 2: 4 × 12
Week 3: 4 × 14
Week 4: 4 × 15
Week 5: Same reps, but 3-second eccentric
```

For most strength exercises, finish sets with approximately **1–3 repetitions in reserve**. The last repetitions should be slow and difficult, but technically controlled.

## The 80/20 priorities

For your exact situation:

1. Keep the gym day heavy.
2. Progress pull-ups and push-ups rather than merely repeating them.
3. Use unilateral kettlebell leg exercises.
4. Eat enough protein while maintaining a moderate calorie deficit.
5. Accumulate walking and rucking without damaging recovery.

That gives you a complete program without needing dips or a large home gym.
